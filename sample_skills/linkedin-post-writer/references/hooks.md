# Hook Formulas

## The 4 Hook Style Taxonomy

Every hook falls into one of four styles. Know which you're writing before you start.

| Style | Definition | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Authority** | Personal challenges or business metrics that establish credibility | When you have a result, transformation, or milestone |
| **Question** | Sparks curiosity or challenges beliefs | When you want the reader to self-assess or confront an uncomfortable truth |
| **Bold** | Controversial, divisive, demands attention | When you have a genuine B&W position you can defend |
| **Fact** | Industry data or news that makes the point for you | When a specific number or real-world event is the strongest argument |

## Hook Editing Principles

**Kaplan's Law**: If a word isn't working for you, it's working against you. Delete anything that doesn't add meaning.

**Harry Dry's 3-part test**: Make it Concrete, Visual, Falsifiable.
- *Concrete* — specific and tangible, not vague
- *Visual* — the reader can picture it
- *Falsifiable* — someone could disagree (if everyone agrees, it's not a real statement)

**Opinions should be B&W**: "Most companies do outreach inefficiently" is gray — nobody disagrees, nobody engages. "Cold email dies for anyone who hasn't changed their approach" is B&W — forces a choice. Gray opinions don't create engagement.

**90% of your post time should go to the hook.** The average reader insta-likes a strong hook and skims the body. The hook is its own piece of content.

---

Hook must land in **first 210 characters**. First sentence under 12 words. No yes/no questions. No "I" as opener. No hashtags.

The "see more" fold = 275 chars on mobile — treat everything before it as your hook zone.

## The 3-Line Hook Anatomy (Lead Magnet Posts)

LinkedIn gives you 3 lines before "see more." These 3 lines determine everything.

**Line 1 — Bold claim or FOMO builder**
"I studied Alex Hormozi's LinkedIn outreach for 72 hours."
The specificity of the number (72, not "a long time") is what makes it feel real. It tells the reader: I've already done the work you'd need to do. By accessing this resource, you skip 72 hours of research.

**Line 2 — The dream outcome**
"And broke down his playbook that grew Acquisition.com to $250M/year."
The scale makes the resource feel premium. Even if someone's goal is a fraction of that, the anchor elevates perceived value.

**Line 3 — The re-hook (after "see more")**
"Everyone is doing LinkedIn outreach wrong."
After they click "see more," this bold contrarian claim keeps them reading. Pattern interrupt after the pattern interrupt.

**Hook formula for lead magnets**: Outcome + timeframe + mechanism
"I booked 1.6K meetings (outcome) in 12 months (timeframe) using LinkedIn (mechanism)."
Adding "it took me 27 hours to put this together" stacks perceived value — if someone invested 27 hours, the resource must be genuinely valuable.

### 1. Specific Result
Format: `[Number/metric] + [timeframe] + [unexpected context]`
> *"AI cut our influencer vetting time by 73% in 6 weeks."*
> *"We analyzed 200 luxury campaigns. The AI-assisted ones outperformed by 2.3x."*

Best for: Case studies, Template C, credibility-building

---

### 2. Myth Buster
Format: `[Common belief] is wrong. [What's actually true.]`
> *"Everyone thinks AI in fashion is about content generation. It's not. It's about signal processing."*
> *"The agencies scaling fastest with AI aren't the most technical. They're the most opinionated."*

Best for: Contrarian takes, thought leadership, Template B

---

### 3. Listicle Tease
Format: `[Number] [things/lessons/mistakes] about [topic] that [outcome]`
> *"5 things luxury brands get wrong about AI influencer scoring — and what we changed."*

Best for: Template B, educational content

---

### 4. Before / After
Format: `[Before state]. Then [trigger]. Now [after state].`
> *"3 years ago: zero AI in production at YKONE. Today: it touches every campaign brief."*

Best for: Template C, Template E, transformation stories

---

### 5. Curiosity Gap
Format: `[Set up a tension or paradox that demands resolution]`
> *"The more we automate at YKONE, the more our clients want to talk to humans. Here's why."*
> *"We deployed AI across our influencer ops. Our strategists got busier, not less."*

Best for: Template E, stories, contrarian angles

---

### 6. Social Proof
Format: `[Specific person/brand/cohort] + [specific result] + [what they did]`
> *"A Paris fashion house gave us 72 hours to audit 1,200 influencers. We used AI. Here's what we found."*

Best for: Template C, authority-building

---

### 7. Direct Address
Format: `If you [role/situation], [specific insight]`
> *"If you're a CMO at a luxury brand still vetting influencers manually, your competitors have 3 months on you."*

Best for: ICP-targeted posts, conversion content (BOF)

---

### 8. Controversial Take
Format: `[Strong, specific, potentially disagreeable claim]`
> *"AI won't replace luxury brand strategists. It'll expose the ones who were never strategists to begin with."*
> *"The influencer industry doesn't have an authenticity problem. It has a measurement problem."*

Best for: Thought leadership, high-engagement takes, driving comments

## Hook Calibration for Sunnyvale Founder Voice

**Do**: Real numbers (reply rates, time savings, MRR), founder observations, B2B outreach specifics, European market angle
**Don't**: Feature lists, generic AI hype, "I'm excited to share", anything a junior associate could write

- Posts with specific $ or % in hook → 2x engagement vs. no numbers
- Contrarian + specific number → highest comment volume
- Vulnerability opener (story format) → highest reaction volume (S-Tier)
- Mistake/cost framing → strong comment triggers ("this may be hard to hear")

## 4 Viral Hook Templates (Reverse-Engineered from 16M+ View Threads)

These are fill-in-the-blank formulas validated across millions of impressions. Adapt the structure to Sunnyvale's voice — keep the psychology, change the topic.

### Template 1 — Contrarian Revelation
**Pattern**: `Every [demographic] blames [common belief] for [problem]. Turns out it's not [belief]... it's [real cause].`

**Why it works**: Challenges a universal belief, creates an "aha moment," promises hope by reframing the problem as solvable.

**Sunnyvale adaptation**:
- "Every BD manager blames bad copy for cold email silence. Turns out it's not the copy... it's asking before giving."
- "Every agency founder blames the market for dry pipelines. Turns out it's not the market... it's the research taking 30 minutes per prospect."

---

### Template 2 — Breaking News + Controversy
**Pattern**: `[New thing] dropped [timeframe] ago. Turns out, it [shocking behavior] to [avoid consequence].`

**Why it works**: Creates urgency ("21 hours ago"), triggers fear/curiosity, positions as insider knowledge.

**Sunnyvale adaptation**:
- "Cold email reply rates dropped below 1.2% this quarter. Turns out, most teams made it worse by adding more 'personalization.'"
- "B2B buyers now receive 120+ sales emails per week. Turns out, the ones getting replies aren't sending more — they're giving first."

---

### Template 3 — Unexpected Authority
**Pattern**: `The most [superlative] [category] in the world today isn't [expected answer]... It's [unexpected reveal].`

**Why it works**: Challenges conventional wisdom, creates mystery, promises exclusive knowledge.

**Sunnyvale adaptation**:
- "The highest-converting cold email sequence I've seen isn't the most personalized one... It's the one that gives something before asking for anything."
- "The best-performing agency BD team I've come across isn't the biggest... It's a founder-seller with 25 seconds and a behavioral profile."

---

### Template 4 — Statistical Shock
**Pattern**: `[Unexpected group] has [shocking stat] compared to [mainstream group]. Their secret? Not [expected solutions] but [simple alternative].`

**Why it works**: Provides stark contrast, challenges modern assumptions, offers simple hope.

**Sunnyvale adaptation**:
- "Agencies using give-before-ask outreach get 18–22% reply rates vs. 3–4% for traditional cold email. Their secret? Not better copy. A deliverable before the ask."
- "The best BD managers I know book 12–15 calls/week from 200 contacts. Their secret? Not more volume. A behavioral profile and one relevant asset."

## The Cliffhanger Technique

Mid-post cliffhangers keep people reading. Create information gaps that demand resolution.

**Formats that work:**
- "His next move?" (after setting up a scenario)
- "But here's what nobody talks about:" (after a common observation)
- "The next one is VERY crucial, and not well known:" (in a listicle)
- "Here's where it gets interesting:" (before a reveal)

Use once or twice in a post. More than that = diluted.

## Specific Number Rules (Applies to All Content)

The brain processes specificity as data. Approximations get filtered as marketing.

- Use exact statistics: 4% not "low percentage", 18,425 not "a lot of steps"
- Include time comparisons: "3–4x more than typical" beats "much more"
- Provide exact measurements: "25 seconds vs. 25 minutes" beats "much faster"
- Show percentage improvements: "63% lower" beats "significantly lower"
- Use time-based rules: "within 5 minutes" beats "quickly"

**Sunnyvale's specific number inventory** (use these in hooks):
- Sub 1.2% → cold email industry reply rate 2026
- 18–22% → lead magnet outreach reply rate
- 25 seconds → SignalScore profile vs. 30–60 min manually
- 120+ → sales emails B2B buyer receives weekly
- 400% → better conversion when contacted within 5 min
- 25+ → tools in the average solo operator stack
- 9.2% → Freckle flywheel LinkedIn reply rate benchmark
